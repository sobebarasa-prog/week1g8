Video: Skimming & Scanning

Explaining what skimming and scanning are, how they are used, and why they are essential reading skills.

1️⃣
What is SKIMMING?

Skimming means reading a text quickly to get the general idea — what is it about? You do NOT read every word. You look at headings, the first and last sentences of paragraphs, and key words.

When do we skim?
• Before reading a long article to decide if it is worth reading
• To get a quick overview of what a text covers
• To remind yourself of a text you read before
How to skim:
1. Read the title and any headings
2. Read the first sentence of each paragraph
3. Look for repeated key words
4. Read the last paragraph
2️⃣
What is SCANNING?

Scanning means moving your eyes quickly through a text to find a specific piece of information — like a name, a date, a number, or a definition. You do NOT read every word — you hunt for the target.

When do we scan?
• When looking for the meaning of an unfamiliar word
• When searching for a specific fact in an article
• When looking at a dictionary or index
How to scan:
1. Know exactly what you are looking for
2. Run your eyes down the page quickly
3. Stop when you find your target word or phrase
4. Read that section carefully
3️⃣
Skimming vs Scanning — Key Differences
SKIMMINGSCANNING
Get the general ideaFind specific information
Read headings & topic sentencesLook for a target word or fact

Skimming Strategy: Read the title → first sentence of each paragraph → last paragraph. Do NOT read every word. Write the main idea in one sentence in your exercise book before answering.
Human Rights: A Foundation for All
Source: Adapted from UNICEF Educational Resources, 2024

Human rights are the fundamental rights and freedoms that every person is entitled to, regardless of their nationality, gender, age, religion, or social status. These rights are protected by international laws and national constitutions around the world.

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights, adopted by the United Nations in 1948, established thirty key rights for all people. These include the right to life, the right to education, the right to freedom of expression, the right to health, and the right to be free from discrimination.

In Kenya, the Constitution of 2010 includes a strong Bill of Rights that protects the rights of every citizen. Children specifically are protected under Article 53, which guarantees them the right to a name, nationality, education, healthcare, and freedom from harmful work or exploitation.

Despite these protections, millions of people around the world — including children — still face violations of their basic rights. Poverty, conflict, and lack of access to quality education remain serious barriers to the full realisation of human rights for all.

Every person has both rights and responsibilities. Respecting the rights of others, speaking up when rights are violated, and learning about the laws that protect us are important ways that young people can contribute to a just and equal society.

3
Scan for Unfamiliar Words — Use the Dictionary
👥 10 PAIRS — scan the same text; each pair finds and defines words

With your partner, scan the information text in Step 2 for words you do not know or are unsure about. Write each word and use a dictionary to find its meaning as used in the text. Each pair finds their own set of words.

Scanning tip: Don't read from the beginning — run your eyes over the text quickly looking for long or unfamiliar words. When you spot one, stop and write it down. Then use the dictionary to find its meaning in this context.
Suggested words to look up from the text:
fundamental, entitled, nationality, constitution, convention, discrimination, exploitation, violation, realisation, responsibilities
— You can also find your own words from the text.

Read the Text Aloud — Human Rights
👤 20 INDIVIDUALS — each learner reads and answers

Read the information text from Step 2 aloud — either to yourself or to a partner. Then select your name and answer the comprehension questions on human rights. Record yourself reading a paragraph if you can.

Reading Aloud Tips:
• Read clearly and at a steady pace — not too fast
• Emphasise key words: rights, freedom, entitled, discrimination
• Pause at punctuation marks
• Pronounce each word carefully — use your scanning work from Step 3 to help
